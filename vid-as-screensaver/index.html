what is a screensaver

A screensaver is a program that takes over your display when the device has been idle for a bit. Originally they existed to prevent burn-in on old CRT monitors — pixels left on the same image for too long would literally burn that image into the screen. Modern displays don't have that problem, but screensavers stuck around because they're useful and look good.

On Android, the feature is called Daydream. It activates when your phone is charging and sitting still — on a dock, on a nightstand, plugged into a charger on your desk. Instead of a blank screen, you get something running. This app makes that something a video of your choice.

what is android daydream

Daydream is Android's built-in interactive screensaver system, available since Android 4.2. It's separate from your regular lock screen wallpaper — it only kicks in when the phone is charging and the screen would otherwise go dark. Google ships a few built-in options like a clock and photo slideshow, but the API is open for any app to use.

To set a Daydream, go to Settings, find Display, then look for Screen Saver or Daydream depending on your Android version. From there you pick which app handles it and when it should activate — while charging, while docked, or both. Once configured, it just works in the background without you thinking about it.

One thing worth knowing: some Android skins (Samsung One UI, Xiaomi MIUI, etc.) bury the Daydream setting or rename it. If you can't find it in Display settings, search the settings app for "screensaver" or "daydream" directly.

why this app exists

There isn't a straightforward video screensaver on the Play Store. There are wallpaper apps, live wallpaper apps, and apps that do something vaguely similar but not quite right. None of them just let you pick a video file and have it play as a Daydream without jumping through extra hoops or paying for a subscription.

This app does one thing: plays a video when your phone is charging and idle. You pick the file, set the scale mode, decide whether to loop and whether to mute, and that's it. No accounts, no cloud sync, no ads, no background services running when you don't need them.

The catalog feature is there for people who don't have a specific video in mind — just a curated set of things that look good on a phone screen. Ambient visuals, nature footage, that kind of thing. If you have something you think belongs in there, open an issue on GitHub.

video format notes

The app uses Android's built-in media player, so anything Android can play, this app can play. MP4 with H.264 video is the safest bet — it works on every Android device going back years. H.265 (HEVC) works on most modern devices but can have issues on older hardware.

For vertical phones, portrait-orientation videos look best. If you use a landscape video, zoom mode will crop the sides, fit mode will add black bars top and bottom, and adapt mode will letterbox it explicitly. All of these are fine depending on what you want.

File size doesn't matter much since the video stays on your device — there's no streaming involved. A 1GB video plays the same as a 50MB one. That said, very high bitrate files on older phones can stutter, so if you notice that, try re-encoding at a lower bitrate.
